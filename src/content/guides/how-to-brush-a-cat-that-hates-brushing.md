## Reduce The Goal

If a cat already dislikes brushing, the first goal is not a perfect coat. The first goal is a calm repeatable interaction. Even thirty seconds of tolerance is useful if you can repeat it several times each week.

## Pick The Right Time

Try brushing after play, after a meal, or during a naturally relaxed part of the day. A cat that is already alert, irritated, or trying to leave the room is much less likely to accept a new grooming routine.

## Keep The Tool Simple

Use one brush or comb that feels gentle and predictable. Switching between several tools in one session often makes the process feel more intrusive. For nervous cats, consistency matters more than owning a full grooming kit.

## Stop Before The Cat Gets Angry

One common mistake is pushing through resistance because you want to finish the job. That usually teaches the cat that brushing means being trapped. End the session while tolerance is still intact, then repeat later.

## Build A Better Pattern

It helps if brushing always follows the same small sequence: call the cat, short contact, brief brushing, treat, done. Predictability reduces tension for both the owner and the cat.
