## Why tracking matters

Litter tracking becomes a much bigger problem in small apartments, multi-cat homes, and rooms with hard floors. A litter that performs well inside the box can still create daily cleanup work if it spreads too easily.

## What usually causes tracking

Tracking is usually influenced by grain size, clumping texture, how much litter sticks to paws, and how the cat exits the box. Long-haired cats and cats that dig aggressively often make the problem worse.

## What to look for

Choose litters that are known for lower stickiness, steadier clumps, and larger granules where possible. The litter box setup matters too. A mat, a larger exit area, and the right box height can reduce mess as much as the litter itself.

## Practical buying advice

Do not judge only by bag price. Low-tracking litter can cost more upfront but save time and reduce wasted litter outside the box. When comparing products, track not only odor control and dust, but also how much cleanup they create around the box.

## Final take

There is no perfect litter for every cat, but low-tracking choices usually come from matching litter type, box setup, and the cat's coat and behavior. Start with one reliable setup, watch what actually happens around the box, and adjust from there.
